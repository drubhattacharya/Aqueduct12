Step 2 of 12 — Compliance Foundation · Module 1

Your Community Health Needs Assessment is the evidentiary foundation for every artifact, ROI projection, and funder application. The IRS Requirement
Section 501(r)(3) of the Internal Revenue Code requires tax-exempt hospitals to conduct a CHNA at least once every three years and adopt an implementation strategy (IS) to address identified needs. The CHNA must be made publicly available. Your NEMT program must be traceable to a documented priority in the CHNA — otherwise, it cannot be reported as a community benefit expenditure on Schedule H (Form 990).
Schedule 990H — Where NEMT Lives
NEMT programs are reported under Schedule H, Part I, Line 7e — Community Health Improvement Services. This is the most scrutinized category because it represents proactive community investment rather than Medicaid shortfall or uncompensated care. The IRS and Senate Finance Committee are specifically examining whether organizations can demonstrate a direct line from a documented CHNA priority to the IS activity and its reported cost. Aqueduct generates this documentation chain automatically.
Transportation as a CHNA Priority
Transportation barriers cut across virtually every health priority identified in modern CHNAs — access to care, chronic disease management, maternal health, mental health. If your CHNA documents transportation as a barrier (even as a secondary finding), it qualifies NEMT for Schedule H reporting. This step extracts that documentation for you.

Step 3 of 12 — Compliance Foundation · Module 1

AKS & Safe Harbor Self-Assessment.

The Anti-Kickback Statute (42 U.S.C. § 1320a-7b(b)) prohibits offering free transportation to induce Medicare or Medicaid utilization. A properly structured NEMT program qualifies under the Non-Monetary Remuneration Safe Harbor — but only if it meets specific design criteria. Complete this assessment before building the financial case.

The Anti-Kickback Statute
The AKS prohibits knowingly offering, paying, soliciting, or receiving anything of value to induce or reward referrals of items or services covered by federal health care programs. Free transportation to patients is technically "remuneration" — value provided to induce utilization. Without Safe Harbor protection, a well-intentioned NEMT program could expose the organization to penalties up to $50,000 per violation plus exclusion from Medicare and Medicaid.
The Non-Monetary Remuneration Safe Harbor (42 CFR § 1001.952(l))
The OIG's Non-Monetary Remuneration Safe Harbor protects free transportation under three conditions: (1) it is not tied to the volume or value of referrals; (2) it is not marketed to patients; and (3) the transportation is to the entity's own facilities for medically necessary services. Programs designed and documented as clinical tools — not marketing programs — are generally well-positioned.
The Civil Monetary Penalties Law and False Claims Act
CMPL exposure arises when payments intended to induce referrals are coded and billed to federal programs. FCA exposure arises when a billing claim contains false representations about the basis for the benefit provided. Both are downstream risks of an AKS-non-compliant program. Documenting clinical necessity for every NEMT trip is the primary mitigation.

Step 4 of 12 — Compliance Foundation · Module 1

Map your NEMT program to TJC and NCQA accreditation standards.

A well-designed NEMT program addresses multiple Joint Commission and NCQA standards simultaneously. Documenting this alignment elevates NEMT from an operational activity to a strategic accreditation asset — and creates a powerful non-financial justification for funding applications.

The Joint Commission — SDOH Screening & CLAS Standards
TJC's R3 Report on Social Determinants (2021) requires organizations to screen patients for social needs, including transportation, and to connect them with appropriate resources. Transportation screening via ICD-10 Z75.3 and structured NEMT referral pathways directly satisfy the screening requirement. The NEMT program also supports TJC's CLAS standards for addressing language, cultural, and access barriers in care delivery.
NCQA Health Plan Standards — SDOH & Access
NCQA's 2024 Health Plan Accreditation standards require documented processes for identifying and addressing member social needs, including transportation. For health systems participating in managed care plans, a documented NEMT program with Z-code tracking contributes to the health plan's compliance posture and can be included in contract negotiations as a value-add service.
Why Funders Care
CRA examiners, foundation program officers, and federal grant reviewers increasingly evaluate whether proposed programs address accreditation standards. A program that simultaneously satisfies IRS 501(r), AKS Safe Harbor, TJC, and NCQA standards is demonstrably more credible than one that addresses only the specific funder's criteria. Step 5 of 12 — Financial Case · Module 2

Build your four-layer ROI model.

The Aqueduct ROI model quantifies four independent revenue streams: FFS contribution margin recovery, coding & documentation uplift, CCM/TCM billing, and VBC earn-back. Enter your program parameters and generate the financial case for board presentation and funder applications.

Layer 0 — FFS Contribution Margin Base
Every prevented no-show is a recaptured visit. Contribution margin = net revenue per visit minus marginal clinical cost. Transportation-attributable no-shows are estimated at 15–30% of total no-shows nationally. The FFS layer is typically available from Month 1 and establishes the baseline program value before any billing optimization.
Layer 1 — Coding & Documentation Uplift (Z75.3)
Z75.3 (Unavailability/inaccessibility of health care facilities) adds approximately $23/encounter in coding uplift via Hierarchical Condition Category (HCC) risk adjustment, improved CPT bundling, and medical complexity documentation. The 90-day Z-code audit typically drives meaningful improvement in coding accuracy for the enrolled cohort.
Layer 2 — CCM & TCM Billing
Patients enrolled in NEMT — by definition complex, high-need, transportation-barrier patients — are prime candidates for Chronic Care Management (CPT 99490/99491, ~$62–$128/month) and Transitional Care Management (CPT 99495/99496, ~$165–$228/episode). Identifying the subset of enrolled patients who qualify and enrolling them in CCM/TCM adds substantial recurring revenue independent of trip volume.
Layer 3 — VBC Earn-Back
For organizations in ACO REACH, Medicare Advantage, or state VBC programs, reduced ED utilization and 30-day readmissions — both measurably improved by NEMT — translate to quality measure performance and earn-back against quality withholds. The VBC layer typically matures at 12–18 months post-launch as quality scores accumulate. Evidence: Balasubramanian 2025 found 32% readmission reduction in NEMT-enabled populations; Shekelle 2022 meta-analysis found 37% no-show reduction.

Step 6 of 12 — Clinical Case · Module 3

Design your patient engagement and adherence architecture.

The COM-B behavioral model identifies three components of behavior change: Capability, Opportunity, and Motivation. Your NEMT program addresses the Opportunity barrier — but long-term adherence requires all three. Select your engagement levers to generate a program-specific engagement protocol.

The COM-B Model
Michie et al.'s COM-B model (2011) is the evidence base for healthcare behavior change interventions. Behavior occurs when Capability (knowledge + skills), Opportunity (physical + social environment), and Motivation (automatic + reflective) align. Transportation removal addresses Physical Opportunity — but patients who don't understand the value of attending appointments (Capability deficit) or who aren't motivated by perceived benefit (Motivation deficit) will still not use the program.
What Evidence Shows
Chaiyachati et al. (Annals IM, 2018) found that ride-sharing NEMT alone reduced no-shows by only 17% — below model assumptions. Programs that combined NEMT with patient activation communication (motivational elements) and care coordinator outreach (social opportunity) achieved 30–37% reductions. The communication protocol you build in this step is what bridges the gap between "rides available" and "appointments kept."
Communication Cadence Evidence
Two-day confirmation + same-day reminder significantly outperforms single-contact approaches. Bilingual outreach improves uptake in LMI populations by 22–31%. Trust-building language ("Your care team has arranged transportation for your appointment") outperforms transactional language ("Your ride is scheduled") in motivating attendance among hesitant patients.

Step 7 of 12 — Clinical Case · Module 4

Define your operations, vendor, and EHR integration model.

The operational design of your NEMT program determines its defensibility, scalability, and clinical integration. Select your vendor model, EHR workflow, and SLA structure — and generate the operational specifications for your funder application narrative.

Vendor Selection Framework
NEMT vendor types range from Medicaid broker/NEMT management companies (LogistiCare/Modivcare, MTM) to rideshare platforms (Lyft Healthcare, Uber Health) to community-based transportation programs. Selection criteria: HIPAA BAA availability, clinical-grade reliability SLAs (≥95% on-time, ≤15 min wait), geographic coverage match, EHR integration capability, and cost structure. Funders often ask whether the vendor has documented quality metrics — plan for this in your SLA from Day 1.
EHR Integration — The Z75.3 Workflow
Embedding the transportation screening and NEMT referral workflow inside the EHR is essential for both compliance and scalability. The minimum viable workflow: (1) Z75.3 auto-suggested at scheduling for flagged patients; (2) NEMT referral order pathway in provider order set; (3) trip confirmation auto-documented in the social history or care plan section; (4) trip completion feeds back to the scheduling system to auto-close the care gap. Epic, Cerner, and Athena all support this via SMART-on-FHIR apps or standard workflow configuration.
SLA Design for Funders
CRA bank partners and foundation funders will ask how you ensure program quality. Your SLA should include: on-time performance threshold (≥95%), complaints resolution timeline (24-hour), cancellation policy, accessibility standards (wheelchair accessible vehicles available within same wait time), and quarterly reporting requirements.
